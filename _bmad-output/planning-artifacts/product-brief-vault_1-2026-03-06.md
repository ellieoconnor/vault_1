# Product Brief: vault_1

<!-- Content will be appended sequentially through collaborative workflow steps -->

## Executive Summary

Vault 1 is a personal health and habit system built by and for one person: its creator.
Designed from lived ADHD experience, it solves the shame-and-spiral pattern that derails
most habit systems — the one where a single bad day, a broken streak, or a red number
becomes a reason to stop entirely.

It does this through two layers: a floor-based daily dashboard that reframes missed
targets as neutral data (not failure), and a Fallout-themed gamification layer that
rewards consistency, limits active focus to what's manageable, and keeps coaching
strategies visible at the moment they're needed most. This isn't a tracker retrofitted
for ADHD — it's built from the inside out by someone who knows exactly how these
systems break down, and exactly what it would take to not break.

---

## Core Vision

### Problem Statement

For people with ADHD, traditional habit trackers don't cause shame — they *enforce* it.
A day over calories becomes a red number. A missed week becomes a broken streak. An
ambitious goal list becomes a list of things not done. The visual language of most apps
amplifies ADHD's worst patterns: all-or-nothing thinking, difficulty returning after a
setback, and goals multiplying until the whole system collapses under its own weight.

Compounding this: coaching works. The strategies are good. But they live in a meeting
once a week, and by Tuesday they're gone — leaving the user to navigate real-world food
and habit decisions without the coaching layer that makes the difference.

### Problem Impact

Without a system that works *with* the ADHD brain, one rough day doesn't stay one rough
day. It becomes evidence of failure, which becomes avoidance of the app, which becomes
weeks off track. The app that was supposed to help becomes another thing that proved
consistency wasn't possible. The strategies the coach gave are forgotten at the exact
moment they'd be useful. And the goal list — which started at 3 — quietly grows to 15
while producing results for none of them.

### Why Existing Solutions Fall Short

- **Cronometer:** Excellent food tracker, but purely data-focused. No coaching layer,
  no system for surfacing strategies, no emotional reframing of difficult days.
- **Habit trackers (Habitica, etc.):** Gamification without ADHD-aware floor logic —
  streaks still break, failure is still visualized, and goals still multiply.
- **Coaching sessions:** The insights are valuable but ephemeral — no external memory
  between sessions.
- **All existing apps:** Built with the assumption that clear data = motivation. For an
  ADHD brain, clear data about failure = reason to stop.

### Proposed Solution

Vault 1 is a two-layer system:

**Layer 1 — Win the Day (Daily Execution):** A dashboard built around floors, not
targets. Below the floor isn't failure — it's neutral data. Progress bars show where
you are, not what you've missed. Coaching strategies (Cheat Codes) are always visible.
The "Day Complete" ritual closes the loop regardless of what the numbers say.

**Layer 2 — Build Your Character (Weekly Strategy):** A Fallout-themed gamification
system where habits literally build a character and a bunker. Active goals are capped
at 3-5 per week — chosen deliberately, mirroring a coaching session's "what are we
focusing on this week?" Active goals earn XP. Consistency is celebrated at 60%+. A
rough week isn't a failure; it's data for Sunday's planning ritual.

The two layers connect: daily wins feed weekly XP, weekly consistency feeds level
progression, and level progression unlocks bunker upgrades that make the game more
rewarding. The whole loop is designed to keep the user in the system through hard weeks,
not just easy ones.

### Key Differentiators

1. **Floor-based success model:** Below floor = neutral, not failure. This single design
   decision removes the shame trigger that breaks most habit systems for ADHD users.
2. **Coaching externalization (Cheat Codes):** Strategies from coaching sessions are
   surfaced at the moment of decision — not buried in notes from last Tuesday.
3. **Active goal constraint:** 3-5 active goals per week isn't a limitation — it's the
   design. Doing less, deliberately, is the strategy.
4. **ADHD-native design:** Not adapted from a neurotypical productivity system. Built
   from lived experience of exactly where these systems break down.
5. **Fallout gamification as identity:** The character isn't a metaphor for productivity
   — it's a metaphor for *self-building*. The bunker fills up the same way the body and
   habits do: slowly, through consistency, one upgrade at a time.

## Target Users

### Primary User

**Elizabeth — Creator, Sole User, and Product Owner**

Elizabeth is in her 30s, has ADHD, and works a desk job with a one-hour daily commute.
She works out three mornings a week starting at 4:45am (Mon–Wed), meal preps on Sundays,
and manages her nutrition using Cronometer with guidance from her coach, Alex. She uses
a Pomodoro timer for both work and personal tasks, and her phone is her primary
interaction point throughout the day — between focus sessions, at lunch, and in the
evenings.

**Context & Environment:**
- Weekday mornings are structured and productive; early workouts set a positive tone
- The 8–10am window (post-commute, at desk) is her primary logging time — she typically
  logs yesterday's food retroactively rather than in real time
- Weekdays have reliable anchors (job, commute, meals); weekends lack structure entirely
- Thu–Fri WFH days are similar in rhythm but involve less movement
- Weekends are the hardest: no immovable commitments, all-or-nothing thinking around
  rest vs. productivity, and higher exposure to unplanned eating situations

**Current System (the patchwork):**
- Cronometer for food tracking (retroactive logging is the norm)
- Alpha Progression for lifting session tracking
- Apple Watch for steps (data exists, not connected to any habit system)
- Coach Alex's strategies via text messages (ephemeral — not surfaced at point of need)
- Bullet journal: occasional, inconsistent
- Everything else: held in her head

**Frustrations with current approach:**
- Coach strategies live in texts, not accessible when a food decision needs to be made
- Data is reviewed after the fact — no system connects daily numbers to weekly goals
- A bad day visually (red numbers in Cronometer) can trigger shame and avoidance
- The goal list in her head is too long; without a limit, nothing gets consistent focus
- Weekly planning feels like "work" during rest time, creating resistance every Sunday

**What Success Looks Like:**
A week where something went wrong — a hard food day, a missed workout — and she stayed
in the app anyway. She came back Sunday, reviewed the week without shame, saw 68%
consistency and felt like that was enough to build on. The coaching advice she always
forgets was there when she reached for a snack. The app didn't make her feel behind — it
made her feel like she was still in the game.

### Secondary Users

**Alex (Coach) — Indirect Stakeholder**

Alex is Elizabeth's nutrition and habits coach. Alex is not a user of Vault 1 and has
no direct interaction with the app. However, Alex's strategies, advice, and weekly
guidance are a primary source of content for the Cheat Codes feature. The app functions
as an external memory layer for coaching insights — surfacing Alex's advice at the
moment it's needed rather than letting it fade between sessions.

No admin, support, or oversight users are in scope. Vault 1 is a single-user system.

### User Journey

**Discovery:** N/A — Elizabeth is building this for herself.

**Onboarding:**
Elizabeth creates her character, sets her calorie/protein/steps targets (the app
calculates floors automatically), and enters her first 3–5 active goals. She types in
her three Cheat Codes from memory (or pulls them from a recent text from Alex). The
first time she opens the dashboard, everything she needs to track is already there. It
takes one Sunday afternoon to set up.

**Core Daily Usage:**
- **8–10am (primary window):** Imports yesterday's Cronometer CSV, logs workout
  completion and steps manually, reviews yesterday's dashboard. Presses "Day Complete"
  if she didn't the night before. Checks today's dashboard to see where she's starting.
- **Pomodoro breaks / lunch:** Quick check of progress bars — calories, protein, steps.
  Cheat Codes visible if a snack decision is happening.
- **Evening:** Optional check-in. Day Complete if not done yet. Mood tag. XP review.

**The "Aha" Moment:**
She has a rough Thursday. Goes over calories, skips the walk, doesn't log until Friday
morning. She opens the app expecting to feel bad. Instead: the floor system shows amber
(below target, not failure), her two other active goals are still green, and she's at
68% consistency for the week. She presses Day Complete. She gets XP for the Day
Complete ritual. The app doesn't call it a failure. She comes back on Sunday and plans
next week. The spiral didn't happen.

**Long-Term Routine:**
The weekly planning ritual becomes Sunday's version of meal prep — something she does
because she knows Monday-her will be grateful. The bunker fills up slowly. Levels go
up. The cheat codes rotate as coaching evolves. The XP history becomes a visual record
of consistency over months, not a graveyard of missed days.

## Success Metrics

### What "Working" Looks Like

Success for Vault 1 is behavioral, not numerical. The app is working when:

- Active goals and coaching strategies are remembered *during the week*, not just
  recalled at Sunday planning
- Food is being pre-logged proactively (same day or day before) rather than
  retroactively caught up days later
- Sunday meal prep and planning happen as routine — not as a "this is the week I
  buckle down" reset
- After a rough day or rough week, the app is opened again — the spiral doesn't happen
- Coping strategies from coaching are being applied at the moment of decision

The core behavioral shift: moving from periodic bursts of motivation to consistent
low-grade engagement. No more "fresh start" Mondays.

### Personal Objectives

**4–6 weeks:**
- Weekly planning ritual completed at least 3 of the first 4 Sundays
- App opened and dashboard checked at least 5 days/week
- Pre-logging rate improving (target: food logged same-day or next-morning for 4+
  days/week)
- At least one rough week navigated without a multi-day gap in app usage

**3 months:**
- Weekly consistency % averaging 65%+ across the month
- Cheat Codes updated and actively referenced (not ignored)
- No shame spiral lasting longer than 2 days before returning
- Sunday planning ritual feels routine, not like a chore

**6–12 months:**
- Measurable progress toward 30 lb outcome goal
- Character at Level 5+ (reflects sustained engagement over time)
- The system feels automatic — planning and logging are habits, not decisions

### Key Performance Indicators

| Metric | Target | How Measured |
|---|---|---|
| Weekly consistency % | ≥ 65% | Calculated in-app each week |
| Day Complete presses | ≥ 5/week | In-app tracking |
| Weekly planning ritual | Completed each Sunday | Boolean per week in-app |
| Days since last login | ≤ 2 (no long gaps) | In-app login tracking |
| Pre-logging rate | 4+ days/week same-day or next-morning | Log timestamp vs. log date |
| Active goal recall | Can name all 3–5 without checking | Self-assessed monthly |

### Failure Conditions to Watch For

- **Gamification gaming:** XP becomes the goal rather than the habit — pressing
  Day Complete without tracking, or logging fake encounters for XP. If this happens,
  the reward structure needs rebalancing.
- **Planning ritual avoidance:** If Sunday planning is consistently skipped, the
  ritual is too heavy. Needs to feel like a 5-minute win, not a work task.
- **Login gaps > 5 days:** Early warning sign of full abandonment. The floor system
  and no-streak-breaking design should prevent this, but it's the key indicator to
  watch.

### Process Over Outcome

Weekly consistency % at 75%+ is a success regardless of scale movement. The outcome
goal (30 lbs) is tracked separately and is a *result* of the process — not the metric
used to judge whether the app is working. A week of 78% consistency with no weight
change is a win. A week of 45% consistency with a 1 lb drop is a warning sign.

## MVP Scope

### Core Features (MVP)

The MVP delivers the complete core loop: daily tracking without shame, coaching
strategies surfaced at point of need, and a weekly planning ritual that prevents the
"fresh start Monday" pattern.

**1. User Setup & Onboarding**
- Simple account creation (username + password, no OAuth needed)
- Target setting: calorie, protein, steps targets
- Floors auto-calculated (calories: target - 250, protein: target × 0.8, steps: target × 0.5)
- Cheat Codes setup (max 3, user-defined coaching strategies)
- Initial active goal selection (3–5 from starter library)

**2. Win the Day Dashboard**
- Manual daily logging: calories, protein, steps, workout checkbox
- Floor-based progress bars with color zones:
  - Amber: below floor (neutral — not failure)
  - Green: floor to target (on track)
  - Blue: above target (bonus)
- Cheat Codes always visible on dashboard
- "Day Complete" button (pressable anytime, not locked behind metrics)
- Mood tagging on Day Complete (7 options)
- Yesterday's summary prompt on login (if prior day not completed)

**3. Available Goals Library**
- User creates and manages their full list of possible goals
- Goal types: daily (tied to a metric) or weekly (frequency-based)
- Edit and delete existing goals

**4. Weekly Active Goal Selection**
- Select 3–5 active goals per week from the library
- Enforced 3–5 limit
- Active goals visible on dashboard (progress tracked)
- Only active goals count toward weekly consistency %

**5. Weekly Planning Ritual**
- Sunday (or user-defined day) planning screen
- Last week's review: each active goal with days completed
- Weekly consistency % with tier labels:
  - 60–74%: "Survived the Wasteland"
  - 75–89%: "Thriving"
  - 90%+: "Elite"
- Goal selection for next week (keep / drop / add)
- App suggests: keep what's working, add 1 new challenge

**6. Fallout Theme + PWA**
- Vault-Tec color palette, retro-futuristic typography
- PWA installable on mobile (manifest + service worker)
- Responsive design (320px–768px)

### Out of Scope for MVP

Deferred to Phase 2 and beyond — these build on the core loop but don't solve
the primary problem.

| Feature | Rationale for Deferral |
|---|---|
| XP, levels, SPECIAL stats | Motivational layer — core loop works without it |
| Bunker progression (visual) | Dependent on leveling system |
| Encounters system | Valuable but not load-bearing for core problem |
| Maintenance Mode | Builds on proven floor system — Phase 2 |
| Cronometer CSV import | Manual logging is lower friction (desktop-only export) |
| Outcome goals + weight tracking | Independent of core loop; emotionally loaded; Phase 2 |
| Character creation (full Fallout) | Simplified user setup covers the need |
| Apple Health integration | Phase 3 — manual steps entry sufficient for MVP |

### MVP Success Criteria

The MVP is successful when:

- Dashboard is checked at least 5 days/week consistently
- Weekly planning ritual completed at least 3 of first 4 Sundays
- At least one rough week navigated without a login gap > 3 days
- Cheat Codes section is being read (not ignored)
- Active goals can be recalled without opening the app

**Go signal for Phase 2:** 4+ weeks of consistent use with 65%+ weekly consistency
average. At that point, the gamification layer and adaptive features unlock as
reward layers on a proven foundation.

### Future Vision (Phase 2+)

**Phase 2 — Adaptive & Gamified Layer:**
- **Maintenance Mode:** A deliberate third state for known rough periods (vacations,
  high-stress work weeks). User activates it proactively — floors adjust downward,
  active goal count can drop to 1–2, and the weekly consistency % is calculated
  against the adjusted targets. Completing Maintenance Mode is marked as a win, not
  a compromise. Key design principle: this must be activated intentionally (not
  retroactively), so it stays a strategic choice rather than a backdoor excuse.
- **Encounters System:** Plan upcoming challenges (dinners out, travel, events).
  5-field form: event name, date, risk, plan, success definition. Earn XP for
  planning; earn more for post-event reflection regardless of outcome. Day-of
  reminder visible on dashboard.
- **XP, levels, SPECIAL stats:** Gamification layer on top of the proven core loop.
  Active goals earn XP; Day Complete earns bonus XP; weekly consistency earns bonus.
- **Bunker progression:** Visual character home base that upgrades with levels.
  Functional bonuses at key levels (e.g., water purifier at Level 2 boosts water XP).
- **Cronometer CSV import:** Optional nutrition import for weeks when desktop access
  is convenient. Merges with manual logs; detects duplicates.
- **Outcome goals + weight tracking:** Long-term goal setting (e.g., lose 30 lbs)
  tracked separately from process goals. Weekly average weight only (hides daily
  fluctuation). Connection shown: "Your active goals are how you're getting there."

**Phase 3 — Depth & Integration:**
- Apple Health integration (steps auto-sync from Apple Watch)
- Companions, weapon unlocks, user-defined real-world rewards tied to levels
- Advanced analytics: mood trends over time, consistency charts, XP history
- Native logging (replace Cronometer dependency entirely)

**Phase 4+ — Potential Expansion:**
- Coach-facing view (Alex can see active goals and cheat codes)
- Apple Watch native app
- Opening to others with similar ADHD + coaching needs
